Chapter 01

What Is QSBS?
Let's Start Simple.

Imagine the government saying: "Invest in small startups, take the risk — and if it pays off, you can keep the profits completely tax-free." That is essentially what QSBS is.

"Think of QSBS as a golden reward for backing the little guy. You bet on a small company early, hold your stake for 5 years, and if it grows — the IRS steps aside and lets you keep up to $10 million of that win, completely free of federal tax."

— Simple Analogy for New Investors

QSBS stands for Qualified Small Business Stock. It is governed by Section 1202 of the Internal Revenue Code (IRC), a law originally passed in 1993 and significantly strengthened in 2010 when Congress increased the exclusion to 100%. The core idea is simple: to encourage investment into small, growing U.S. businesses by offering extraordinary tax benefits to investors who take the early risk.

📜

The Law Behind It

Section 1202 of the U.S. Internal Revenue Code. Enacted in 1993, enhanced in 2010. It specifically rewards patient, long-term investors in small startups.

🎯

The Purpose

Congress created QSBS to funnel private investment into small businesses — the engine of the U.S. economy — by making early-stage investing extremely tax-efficient.

💡

Who Benefits

Angel investors, startup founders, early employees with stock options, and venture capital investors. Anyone who invests in qualifying startups at an early stage.

🏛️

Federal vs. State

The 100% exclusion applies to federal taxes. Individual states have their own rules — some follow the federal law, others do not (like California).

💰

The $10M Rule

You can exclude the greater of $10 million in gains OR 10 times your original investment from federal tax. The more you invest early, the bigger your potential exclusion.

🌍

Who Can Invest

Any individual, trust, or estate that buys original stock directly from a qualifying U.S. C-Corporation. Corporate investors do not qualify — this benefit is for people, not companies.

Chapter 02

How Does QSBS Work?
Step by Step.

The process has four distinct stages — from investing to collecting your tax-free gains. Here's how it unfolds in real life.

1

You Invest in a Small Business (C-Corp)

You purchase original stock (not on a secondary market) in a U.S. C-Corporation. The company must have gross assets of $50 million or less at the time you invest. You can invest as an individual or through certain pass-through entities like partnerships. The stock must be acquired for cash, property, or services — not purchased from another shareholder.

Key requirement: Must be an original issuance
2

The Company Operates in a Qualifying Industry

Not all businesses qualify. QSBS applies to startups in technology, retail, manufacturing, scientific research, and most general industries. It does NOT apply to professional service firms (law, accounting, consulting, financial services, health services, hospitality, or entertainment). The company must also be an active business — not a holding company.

Excluded: law, finance, health, hospitality, consulting
3

You Hold the Stock for at Least 5 Years

This is the patience test. You must hold the stock for a minimum of 5 years without selling. If you sell even a day before the 5-year mark, you lose the entire benefit. There is a partial exclusion for earlier sales (50% for stock acquired before Feb 2009, 75% between Feb 2009 and Sept 2010), but the full 100% exclusion only kicks in after 5 years of continuous ownership.

Partial exclusions exist for pre-2010 acquisitions
4

You Sell and Claim the Exclusion

When you sell after 5+ years, you can exclude from federal income tax the greater of: $10 million in gain, OR 10 times your original investment (adjusted basis). You report the sale on your tax return but exclude the qualifying gain. The gain that is excluded does NOT appear in your taxable income for federal purposes. Your CPA reports this on Form 8949 and Schedule D.

Greater of: $10M or 10x adjusted basis

Chapter 03

Who & What Qualifies?
The Checklist.

QSBS has specific rules — for both the investor and the company. Here's a detailed breakdown of every requirement you need to meet.

Company Requirements

  • ✓
    Must be a U.S. C-CorporationOnly C-Corps qualify. LLCs, S-Corps, partnerships, and sole proprietorships do NOT qualify for QSBS treatment — this is the #1 reason deals fail to qualify.
  • ✓
    Gross Assets Under $50 MillionAt the time the stock is issued (and immediately after), the company's total gross assets must not exceed $50 million. This ensures only genuinely "small" businesses qualify.
  • ✓
    Active Business RequirementAt least 80% of the company's assets must be used in active conduct of a qualifying business. The company cannot be a passive holding company or investment vehicle.
  • ✗
    Not a Professional Service BusinessCompanies in law, accounting, health, financial services, brokerage, insurance, engineering, architecture, consulting, athletics, performing arts, or hospitality are EXCLUDED.
  • ✗
    Not a Hotel, Restaurant, or FarmCertain businesses like hotels, motels, restaurants, and farming operations are specifically excluded from QSBS qualification.

Investor Requirements

  • ✓
    Must Be a Non-Corporate TaxpayerThe exclusion is available to individual investors, trusts, and estates. C-Corporations buying stock in other C-Corps do NOT get the QSBS benefit.
  • ✓
    Original Issuance OnlyYou must acquire stock directly from the company (original issuance), not by purchasing shares from another investor on a secondary market.
  • ✓
    Acquired for Cash, Property, or ServicesStock received as compensation for services rendered (like employee stock options) can qualify, as can stock purchased for cash or contributed property.
  • ✓
    Hold for 5+ Years ContinuouslyThe holding period must be continuous. If you gift, transfer, or sell even a portion, it can affect the holding period for that portion of shares.

Pro Tip from a CPA Perspective: Always verify QSBS eligibility BEFORE investing — not after. Once you invest in the wrong type of entity (like an LLC), you cannot retroactively qualify for QSBS. This is one of the most common and costly mistakes early-stage investors make.
